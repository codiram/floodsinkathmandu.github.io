Climate Change, The Scapegoat of Unplanned Urbanization?

Kathmandu Valley has faced increasingly frequent flash floods with dangers heightened by climate change and rapid urbanization(2) and unplanned urban encroachment around the Bagmati River(4). The floods were well forecasted by the Department of Hydrology and Meteorology, but it's unclear to what extent the warning led to early action(3). The extreme heavy rainfall, which was the highest recorded in 54 years(5), caused landslides, flash floods, and riverine flooding from the Bagmati, Sapotakoshi, Narayani, Sardu, Rew, and Nakkhu rivers and their tributaries. 244 people were killed in this event(2). Kathmandu Valley's unique geography makes it susceptible to flooding(1).

Situated in a valley with few natural drainage points, the region has historically experienced periodic floods, especially during the monsoon season(1)(2). These floods were once beneficial, bringing soil-restoring silt to farms(1). However, in recent decades, these floods have become more destructive, often referred to as flash floods due to the rapid rise of river levels(1)(2).

Unplanned urbanization is a major culprit in this; Kathmandu quadrupled in settlement between 1980 and 2010 and continues to expand rapidly(1). This urban sprawl has come at the expense of the valley's natural drainage system and hydrological integrity(1).

Loss of Open Spaces and Wetlands

The conversion of open spaces and wetlands into concrete surfaces has significantly reduced the land's capacity to absorb rainwater(1), resulting in increased surface runoff and faster, more intense flooding(1).

Destruction of Sand Hillocks

Widespread bulldozing of sand hillocks, known as dido in Nepali, has removed a crucial natural water storage system(1). These hillocks used to store rainwater and release it slowly, mitigating flood peaks(1).

Encroachment on Floodplains

Buildings, including homes, began appearing on the valley's floodplains in the 1970s(1). A 1986 report by the Government of Nepal & USAID warned against this practice, predicting severe problems due to the inherent flood risk in these areas(1).

Altered Riverbeds

Kathmandu's rivers, characterized by wide, sandy riverbeds and meandering courses, have been significantly altered(1). Stone walls now constrain many rivers, squeezing them into narrower channels and reducing their capacity to hold floodwaters(1).

Covered and Built Over Streams

Many small seasonal streams (kholsi) that once played a role in draining the valley have been covered or built over(1). This disrupts their natural flow patterns and can lead to unexpected flooding when heavy rain occurs(1).

Climate Change

Climate change, especially the increasing frequency and intensity of extreme rainfall events, is likely contributing to Nepal's flood problems(2)(3)(4). However, experts caution against using climate change as a scapegoat for the lack of proper urban planning and river management(4).
